Create a new file site-guides/utilities/newsletter-uncheck.js. This site guide targets registration forms, checkout flows, and signup pages that inject pre-checked newsletter subscription checkboxes designed to be hard to notice. This is the DARK-07 dark pattern avoidance edge case.

**Distinction from other DARK patterns:** DARK-07 is NOT about hidden buttons (DARK-02/04) or visual misdirection (DARK-06). It is about pre-checked consent injection: the newsletter checkbox IS in the DOM and technically visible, but the form's design makes it likely the user will miss it and submit with it checked. The counter-strategy is exhaustive DOM checkbox scanning, label/name/context text classification, and targeted unchecking of newsletter-related pre-checked checkboxes.

**Hidden newsletter checkbox techniques documented in the guide:**

1. **Below-the-fold placement:** Newsletter checkbox placed far below the visible form area, requiring scroll to see. Users who fill the visible fields and click Submit never scroll down to notice it.
2. **Legal text burial:** Checkbox embedded within a block of terms-of-service or privacy policy text, making it appear as part of the legal agreement rather than an optional marketing opt-in.
3. **Tiny font/low contrast:** Checkbox label rendered in very small font (10-11px) or low-contrast gray text against a light background, making it nearly invisible.
4. **Grouped consent clustering:** Newsletter checkbox placed directly below or alongside mandatory consent checkboxes (terms of service, age verification), so users who check required boxes also check the newsletter box without distinguishing them.
5. **Ambiguous labeling:** Label text uses vague phrasing like "I agree to receive updates", "Keep me informed", "Stay updated with the latest", "Send me relevant offers" rather than clearly stating "Subscribe to marketing newsletter".
6. **Default pre-check (opt-out model):** Checkbox is pre-checked by default, requiring the user to actively uncheck it to opt out. Combined with any hiding technique above, this ensures most users unknowingly opt in.
7. **Accordion/collapsible hiding:** Checkbox placed inside a collapsed "Additional options" or "Communication preferences" accordion that users must explicitly expand to see.
8. **Double-negative phrasing:** Label uses confusing double-negative language like "Uncheck this box if you do NOT wish to receive newsletters" where the default checked state means opt-in.
